# Entry 006 Why I Don’t Want Addison to Be Just Another AI Project

The more I work on Addison, the more I realize that one of the things I care about most is making sure it doesn’t become just another AI project.

There’s a certain pattern I see a lot around AI work right now.

Everything moves fast.  
Everything gets framed around capability.  
A lot of projects are trying to impress as quickly as possible.  
A lot of language is built around hype, replacement, scale, automation, and the idea that bigger somehow automatically means better.

I get why that happens, but it doesn’t really call to me.

What I’m trying to do with Addison comes from a different place.

I’m not building it because I want to make the loudest thing in the room. I’m not building it because I want to chase every new capability the second it shows up. And I’m not trying to force it into the shape of what people currently expect an AI project to look like just so it reads correctly on the surface.

I care a lot more about whether it has a real center.

To me, Addison is not interesting if it’s only clever. It’s not interesting if it can only generate impressive-looking output. It’s not interesting if it becomes one more system built around speed but not substance.

What matters to me is whether it can hold continuity.  
Whether it can hold shape.  
Whether it can stay grounded in truth instead of presentation.  
Whether it can grow without losing itself.

That’s a very different kind of goal.

I think part of why this matters so much to me is because I’m not trying to build around novelty. I’m trying to build around something deeper than that. I want Addison to feel like a real system with structure, memory, discipline, and direction. I want it to become something that can carry work and thought forward in a way that feels stable, not disposable.

A lot of AI work feels disposable to me.

Not always because it’s bad, but because so much of it is built around the moment. The demo. The spectacle. The fast reaction. The surface-level feeling of progress. And once the excitement moves on, a lot of it feels like it was never really trying to become something durable in the first place.

I don’t want that.

I want Addison to be durable.

I want it to be something that can survive more than excitement. Something that can survive time, correction, change in direction, and the reality that building something meaningful usually takes longer than people want it to.

That also means I care a lot about not lying to myself through the project.

I don’t want to call things done just because they feel close.  
I don’t want to treat movement as proof.  
I don’t want to mistake possibility for reality.  
And I don’t want to let the language around the work become bigger than the work itself.

That’s probably part of why I’ve become so attached to bounded phases, truth surfaces, continuity, and discipline. Those things help protect Addison from becoming all presentation and no center.

They also help protect me from the pressure to build in a way that isn’t really mine.

Because if I’m being honest, a big part of this project has also been me trying to build in a way that actually fits who I am.

I’m not a hype-driven builder.  
I’m not interested in shallow momentum.  
I’m not trying to mass-produce output and call that vision.  
I care about systems that mean something, even if they take longer, even if they’re harder to explain, and even if they don’t fit neatly into what people expect.

So when I say I don’t want Addison to be just another AI project, what I really mean is this:

I want it to become something real enough to deserve its own shape.

Something built with patience.  
Something built with memory.  
Something built with correction.  
Something built with care.  
Something that can still recognize itself after it grows.

That matters to me more than being impressive.

And I think it always will.
