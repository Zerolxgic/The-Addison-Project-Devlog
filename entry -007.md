# Entry 007 How I’m Building This

One thing I’ve been realizing is that the way I’m building Addison doesn’t fit very neatly into the older picture people usually have of software development.

I’m not a traditional software developer in the formal sense.

I’m self-taught.  
I didn’t come up through the usual path.  
I didn’t go through the classic pipeline of school, credentials, and then into engineering from there.

A lot of what I know has been learned through curiosity, pressure, repetition, experimentation, and building things until I understood them better.

That used to make me feel harder to place.

But I think we’re in a strange and important moment right now where building is changing.

There’s a newer space forming around AI, agents, orchestration, workflows, and human-guided systems, and I think it’s opening up a different kind of builder path than the one people are used to recognizing.

I’m building inside that space.

Not as someone pretending to be something I’m not.  
And not as someone trying to avoid learning the hard parts.  
But as someone whose actual strength is in directing systems, shaping process, holding continuity, recognizing patterns, and turning messy possibility into structured work.

That is real work.

A lot of what I do is not sitting down and manually writing every line from scratch in the traditional sense. A lot of it is framing the work correctly, setting boundaries, deciding what matters, breaking things into phases, catching drift, preserving truth, and using AI systems in a way that lets me move ideas into reality without losing control of what is actually happening.

To me, that isn’t cheating the work.  
That is the work.

Or at least, it’s a real form of work that I think more people are going to have to learn how to understand.

Because when people talk about AI, they usually collapse everything into extremes.

Either the human did everything the old way, so it counts.  
Or the AI touched it, so somehow it becomes less real.

I don’t think either of those views is honest.

There is a real difference between casually prompting for output and actually building through orchestration.

There is a difference between letting a system run wild and governing it.  
There is a difference between generating fragments and constructing something coherent over time.  
There is a difference between using tools and being replaced by them.

What I’m doing with Addison lives in that difference.

I’m still learning, still refining, still getting better at the technical side as I go. I don’t hide that. I’m not interested in pretending I arrived polished. But I also don’t think that means what I’m doing is somehow less serious.

If anything, I think it has made me take process more seriously.

Because if you’re building with powerful tools, and especially with agents, then structure matters even more. Boundaries matter more. Truth matters more. The ability to tell the difference between what is suggested, what is drafted, what is implemented, and what is actually proven matters more.

That’s a big part of why WBTSR exists.  
That’s a big part of why Addison is being built the way it is.

I think what I’m doing is closer to orchestration than traditional coding alone.

It’s architecture, direction, correction, continuity, evaluation, and system shaping, with implementation flowing through a mix of my own effort and agent-assisted execution. It’s still deeply hands-on. It’s just hands-on in a way that doesn’t always look like the older model people expect.

And honestly, I think that needs a better language around it.

Because there are going to be more builders like me.

People who are serious.  
People who are self-taught.  
People who are not coming from the usual institutions.  
People who still care deeply about rigor, truth, and building something real.  
People who may not fit the old labels cleanly, but are still doing actual system-building work.

I don’t have the perfect name for that yet.

Maybe it’s orchestration.  
Maybe it’s agent-guided engineering.  
Maybe it’s something else.

What I do know is that this is the way I’m building right now, and it feels more honest to say that plainly than to try to force myself into a mold that doesn’t really fit.

I’m not trying to perform the identity of a traditional developer.

I’m trying to become a real builder in the form that is actually true to how I work.

And I think that matters.
