# Entry 008 Why I Keep the Human in the Loop

One of the clearest things I’ve learned while building Addison is that I do not want the human removed from the center of the work.

That probably goes against the direction a lot of people instinctively want to push AI systems.

A lot of the energy around this space is about reducing human involvement as much as possible. Full autonomy. Less friction. Less oversight. More speed. More delegation. More of the system deciding for itself.

I understand the appeal.

But that isn’t what I want.

For me, keeping the human in the loop is not about fear. It’s not about distrusting the tools. And it’s not about refusing progress. It’s about understanding where responsibility actually belongs.

When you’re building something real, especially something that is supposed to hold continuity over time, authority matters.

Direction matters.  
Judgment matters.  
Interpretation matters.  
The ability to say “this is true,” “this is ready,” or “this crossed a boundary” matters.

I don’t think those things should be handed away carelessly.

AI can generate, assist, suggest, reshape, accelerate, and help make the work possible in ways that would have been much harder before. That part is real. I’m building with that reality every day. But help is not the same thing as authority.

That distinction matters a lot to me.

Because once you stop respecting that distinction, it becomes very easy to blur together suggestion and decision, output and truth, movement and progress. The system starts looking more capable on the surface, but the human can quietly lose their footing underneath it. And once that happens, it gets harder to tell who is actually steering and harder to tell what parts of the work are truly grounded.

I don’t want that for Addison.

I want a system that can become more capable over time without becoming vague about where judgment lives.

That’s a big part of why I keep the human in the loop.

Not because I want the human doing every small thing manually.
Not because I think slowness is automatically better.
But because I think the human should remain the governing layer.

The one who decides what the work is.
The one who decides what is in scope.
The one who accepts or rejects.
The one who carries responsibility for what becomes real.

That matters even more to me because I know how easy it is to be impressed by output.

AI can make something sound complete long before it is complete.
It can make things feel coherent before they are fully grounded.
It can generate momentum fast enough that you can mistake acceleration for clarity.

So keeping the human in the loop is, in part, how I protect the work from that.

It gives me a place to stop and ask:
What actually happened here?
What was suggested?
What was implemented?
What was verified?
What belongs to the system, and what still belongs to me?

Those questions are not obstacles to the work.

They are part of the work.

I think that’s one of the deeper reasons this matters so much to me. I’m not trying to build a system that replaces the need for human judgment. I’m trying to build a system that can work with human judgment without eroding it.

That’s a different goal.

It means I care a lot about boundaries.
It means I care a lot about approval, review, correction, and truth.
It means I care about making sure the system can assist without quietly becoming the unquestioned authority over its own output.

And honestly, I think that matters beyond just my project.

Because the more powerful these systems get, the easier it will be for people to hand over judgment simply because the system is fast, persuasive, or useful. But usefulness and authority are not the same thing. Capability and wisdom are not the same thing. Output and responsibility are not the same thing.

I want Addison to grow without collapsing those differences.

That’s why I keep the human in the loop.

Not as a ceremonial step.
Not as a lack of ambition.
But as a way of keeping the work honest while it becomes more capable.

To me, that isn’t a limitation.

It’s part of the foundation.
