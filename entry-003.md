# Entry 003  Why WBTSR Had to Exist

As I kept building Addison, I started realizing something pretty important:

I didn’t just need a system I could trust.  
I needed a way of working that I could trust too.

That’s where WBTSR came from.

WBTSR stands for **Work, Build, Truth, Session, Rehydrate**.

It didn’t start as some polished framework or big planned methodology. It came out of necessity. It came out of me running into the same problems over and over again while trying to build something that was bigger than a single coding session, bigger than a single burst of motivation, and definitely bigger than what I could safely keep in my head.

I needed a way to stop things from turning into chaos.

One of the biggest problems with building with AI is that it becomes very easy to move fast without actually staying grounded. You can generate a lot, change a lot, and make things look like progress very quickly. But progress and movement are not the same thing. And something looking finished is not the same thing as something being true.

That mattered a lot to me.

I started realizing that if I didn’t have some kind of discipline around the work, I was going to end up with a pile of output that looked impressive on the surface but didn’t really hold together underneath. I didn’t want that. I didn’t want Addison to become one of those projects that grows through momentum but loses its shape.

So over time, I started building rules around how I worked.

Work had to be bounded.  
Build had to be inspectable.  
Truth had to be checked.  
Sessions had to close cleanly.  
And the next session had to be able to start without me pretending I would just remember everything.

That eventually became WBTSR.

What I like about it is that it slows me down in the right ways. It keeps me from treating ideas like results. It keeps me from blurring together what I want to be true and what has actually been proven. It makes me externalize things instead of trusting memory and momentum. And honestly, it protects me from myself a little bit too, because I’m the kind of person who can see ten steps ahead and feel tempted to start living in step ten before step two is even solid.

WBTSR helps me stay honest.

That’s really why it had to exist.

It isn’t there to make the work feel formal. It isn’t there to make things look smart. It exists because I needed a way to keep the project real while it was growing. I needed something that could hold structure around the work the same way Addison was trying to hold continuity around thought.

So in a way, Addison and WBTSR grew together.

One became the system I was building.  
The other became the way I could build it without losing the thread.

And at this point, I don’t really think I could separate them cleanly.  
Not in terms of how they came to life.

WBTSR exists because I needed a way to build that didn’t collapse under complexity, excitement, or drift.

It had to exist because I had to keep going, and I needed a way to keep going honestly.
